Glossary

Credit Card

A financial tool allowing its holder to purchase goods and services on credit, repaying the amount spent later. The credit card is a key symbol of modern consumption, enabling immediate purchases in exchange for deferred payments.

Dematerialization

The process by which physical objects, like credit cards, are replaced by digital versions. This includes the use of mobile payments, digital wallets, and payment apps, eliminating the need for physical cards.

Biometrics

A set of technologies enabling the identification of a person through their biological characteristics (fingerprint, facial recognition, iris, etc.). In the context of payments, biometrics provides enhanced security, replacing traditional methods such as PIN codes.

Cryptocurrencies

Digital currencies, decentralized and based on blockchain technology, enabling transactions without traditional financial institutions. They could eventually replace or complement credit cards in certain types of transactions.

Blockchain

The underlying technology of cryptocurrencies, allowing financial transactions to be securely stored on a decentralized and tamper-proof ledger. Blockchain promises to transform the payments landscape by providing alternatives to credit card networks.

Consumerism

A socio-economic trend encouraging the purchase of goods and services in large quantities, often for non-essential needs. Consumerism is central to the analysis of *Fight Club*, which critiques how credit cards facilitate this lifestyle.

Debt

A financial situation where an individual or entity owes money to a creditor. Personal debt, often exacerbated by excessive credit card usage, is a central issue in the social critiques of films like *Fight Club*.

David Fincher

An American director known for his films exploring human psychology, manipulation, and power dynamics. His works, such as *Fight Club* and *The Social Network*, often address themes related to money, consumption, and power.

Conspicuous Consumption

The practice of purchasing expensive goods to display wealth and social status. This is a recurring theme in Fincher's work, notably in *Fight Club*, where characters question the importance of possessions in their identity.

Social Critique

The analysis or denunciation of negative aspects of society, often related to themes like inequality, capitalism, or exploitation. Films by David Fincher, as well as works like *In Time* and Pink Floyd's song "Money," contain social critiques tied to money and consumption.

Time Currency

A central concept in the film *In Time*, where lifespan is used as a currency. This concept serves as a metaphor for economic inequalities, showing how control over resources—and by extension credit cards in our society—can become a form of power.

Apple Pay / Google Pay / Samsung Pay

Digital payment services enabling transactions without a physical credit card, via a smartphone or smartwatch. They symbolize the shift towards a future where payments are entirely dematerialized.
